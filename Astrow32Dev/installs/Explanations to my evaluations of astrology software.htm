Polar latitudes: Can the program handle the special problems occuring in charts calculated for polar latitudes (north of northern polar circle, and south of the southern polar circle)? 
(This text refers to northern polar latitudes.)

At some times during the day the Ascendant/Descendant axis and the MC/IC axis coincides at polar latitudes. At that moment the ascendant reverses its direction (from direct to retrograde motion, or from retrograde to direct motion).

Test data used for the following example:
3 may 1950, 03:45 EET AM (01:45 UT AM), 67°29' North, 26°40' East (a place in Finland).
The ascendant
Definition: The point of the ecliptic rising above the horizon. This point is always somewhere between north and south on  the eastern side of the meridian.
Action required by the program: The ascendant jumps to the opposite zodiac sign at the moment the Ascendant/Descendant axis and the MC/IC axis coincides.  (Test data Asc = 7°16' Aquarius.)
MC
The behaviour of  MC depends on your exact definition of what it is:
Definition 1: MC is the intersection of the meridian circle with the ecliptic above the horizon. 
Action required by the program: MC jumps to the opposite zodiac sign at the moment the Ascendant/Descendant axis and the MC/IC axis coincide. (Test data MC: 3°03' Cancer.)
Consequences: During the period of the ascendant's retrogradation  MC will in the north on the other side of the north pole  (e.g. the sun's zodiacal degree when the midnight sun is up), and MC will be the point of the zodiac having reached its lowest point in the sky. The advantage of this is that MC will always be above the horizon.
Definition 2: MC is the point of the ecliptic culminating (reaching its highest point in the sky on that place). 
Action required by the program: MC remain in its sign when the Ascendant/Descendant axis and the MC/IC axis an coincide. (Test data MC = 3°03' Capricorn.)
Consequences: The MC will be below and IC abode the horizon even when the midnight sun is crossing the meridian circle. This point always is the intersection between the meridian and ecliptic in the south. The advantage of this is that it is the point of the ecliptic having reached its highest point in the sky, and it is always in the south.
Either defintion can be justified from a theorethichal point of view. What you have to do is to choose what is most important for the symbolic meaning of MC (aspiration, career,position in the world, etc):
  1. Being above the horizon (even though it sometimes will be in the north at polar latitudes). 
  2. Reaching its highest point (even though it sometimes will be below the horizon at polar latitudes). 

My preference: Definition 1 for MC because I think being above the horizon has more to do with the symbolic meaning of MC than reaching its highest point.

Useability: Easyness
How easy it is to find the required function and use it?
How easy it is to enter data (birth data etc)?
How easy it is to get access to and understand the results of the calculations?
Is it possible to understand all this from the program's layout, design of menues, naming of functions etc without having to read a manual?

The ideal is to get the feeling that it is self-evident to perform certain actions to make program do what you wish. It shall not be necessary to make a lot of guess-work och manual-reading to find the right way through a maze of pulldown menues, hard-to-understand-option etc.

For which category of users the program is suitable
To the casual user small shortcomings are of no practical importance, because he/she only wish to do simple things such as calculate a natal chart.
To the specialist and professional astrologer the program of course must meet quite high standards.
Some astroware obviously belongs to one to those cathegories, and in that case I mention it.
